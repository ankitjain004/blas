01From Data to Signals: Encapsulation

When you send a message, it doesn't leave your machine as "text." It's wrapped, layer by layer, each adding a header — like nesting envelopes. This is the network stack at work.

Your data:        "GET /account"
      |  wrap with TLS  -> encrypted payload
      v
   + TCP header   (ports, sequence #, so it arrives reliably and in order)
      v
   + IP header    (source IP, destination IP — for routing across the world)
      v
   + Link header  (MAC addresses — for the next physical hop)
      v
   ===> becomes a stream of BITS: 1010 1100 0011 ...
Key idea: by the time it leaves your network card, your message is just a sequence of 1s and 0s. The physical layer's only job: turn those bits into something that can travel — and turn it back at the other end.

02Modulation: Bits onto a Physical Wave

Modulation = encoding bits onto a physical signal — voltage, light, or radio waves — that can travel through a medium. Demodulation is reading them back at the far end. (That's literally what a "modem" does: MOdulate / DEModulate.)
MediumCarries bits asUsed for
Copper wireVoltage levels (high/low)Ethernet, old phone lines
Radio / airElectromagnetic waves (freq/amplitude)WiFi, cellular, satellite
Optical fiberPulses of light (on = 1, off = 0)Internet backbone, undersea
Bits:    1     0     1     1     0
Light:  ON    off   ON    ON    off    <- a laser flashing billions of times/sec

03"Through the Air" vs Through Glass

People imagine data flying "through the air." That's only true for the very first and very last hop. The long-haul middle is overwhelmingly light through glass fiber.

[Your phone] ~~radio~~> [WiFi router / cell tower]   <- AIR: first hop only
                              |
                          fiber/copper to ISP
                              |
                  ====== FIBER BACKBONE ======        <- light through glass
                              |
                  ==== UNDERSEA FIBER CABLE ====       <- across oceans
                              |
                          fiber to data center
                              |
                        [Server / data center]        <- usually wired, no radio
Common misconception: "the cloud" and WiFi make it feel wireless, but radio is short-range and shared. Essentially all intercontinental traffic is physical glass cable on the ocean floor, not satellites.

04Undersea Cables: The Real Internet

  • ~550+ submarine cables carry roughly 99% of intercontinental data — not satellites.
  • The fiber bundle is about as thick as a garden hose; the glass strands inside are thinner than a human hair.
  • Repeaters every ~80 km re-amplify the light signal (it weakens over distance), powered by a copper conductor running the cable's length.
  • Laid across ocean floors by specialized ships over months.
Faults are physical: the biggest causes are ship anchors and fishing trawlers dragging the seabed near coasts. (Shark bites are real but rare and overhyped.) A major cut can reroute or slow a region's traffic until a repair ship splices it.
Why it matters for design: the internet is physical infrastructure with real geography. A request from Mumbai to a US server must physically cross an ocean — no software trick removes that distance.

05Optical Fiber: Core, Cladding & Light

How does light travel kilometers down a thin, often bending strand of glass without leaking out the sides? Two layers of glass and one elegant physics trick.

   cross-section of a fiber:

      +-----------------------------+
      |        CLADDING (glass)     |   lower refractive index
      |   +---------------------+   |
      |   |   CORE (glass)      |   |   higher refractive index
      |   |   ~~~ light path ~~ |   |   <- light is trapped in HERE
      |   +---------------------+   |
      |        CLADDING (glass)     |
      +-----------------------------+

Core

The thin central glass that actually carries the light. It has a higher refractive index (light travels slightly slower in it).

Cladding

An outer glass layer with a lower refractive index. Its job: reflect escaping light back into the core. It's a mirror made of glass, not metal.

Refractive indexA measure of how much a material slows light. Light bends when crossing between materials of different index — and beyond a certain angle, it reflects entirely instead of crossing. That total reflection is what traps the signal.

06Total Internal Reflection

Total Internal Reflection (TIR) = when light inside the denser core hits the boundary with the cladding at a shallow enough angle (beyond the "critical angle"), 100% of it reflects back in — none escapes. This is the same effect that makes the underside of a swimming pool's surface look like a mirror from below. 
   CLADDING (lower index)
  ────────●────────────────●───────────  boundary
          /\\              /\\            light bounces, never escapes
         /  \\            /  \\
   laser●    \\          /    \\
         \\    \\        /      \\
  ─────────●────●──────●────────────────  boundary
   CLADDING (lower index)

  Light zig-zags down the core via repeated total internal reflections,
  staying trapped even as the fiber bends around corners.
Why it bends around corners: as long as the bend is gentle enough that light still hits the wall beyond the critical angle, TIR keeps working — so fiber can be coiled and routed through buildings and ocean trenches.

07Modal Dispersion & Single-Mode Fiber

The zig-zag has a downside. Light rays bouncing at steeper angles travel a longer total path than rays going nearly straight — so they arrive at slightly different times, smearing the pulse.

Modal dispersionDifferent light paths ("modes") arrive at different times, blurring a sharp 1/0 pulse into a fuzzy one. Over long distances this limits how fast you can send bits before pulses overlap.

THE FIX — SINGLE-MODE FIBER

Multi-mode (wide core)

Core ~50 µm. Many zig-zag paths → modal dispersion. Cheap, short range (within a building/campus).

Single-mode (narrow core)

Core ~9 µm — so thin light travels essentially one straight path. No modal dispersion → very long range. Used for the backbone & undersea cables.

Intuition: make the hallway so narrow there's only one way to walk down it — everyone arrives together, the pulse stays sharp.

08The Speed of Light Is a Hard Floor

Light in glass travels at about 200,000 km/s (~⅔ of its speed in vacuum, because the core slows it). That's fast — but the planet is big, and this sets a floor no engineering can beat.

PathApprox round-trip latency
Same city / same data center< 1–5 ms
London ↔ New York~70–80 ms RTT
Halfway around the globe (one-way)~100+ ms (light alone, before routing/queuing)

HUMAN PERCEPTION THRESHOLDS

  • < ~100 ms — feels "instant" to a human (web clicks, page loads).
  • < ~20 ms — needed for real-time gaming, voice/audio, live music.
  • Above these, interactions start to feel laggy regardless of how fast the server is.
The hard truth: real latency is always worse than the light-speed floor — add TCP/TLS handshakes, router hops, queuing, and processing. You can't go faster than physics; you can only move the data closer.

09Why CDNs & Regional Data Centers Exist

Since geography is a fixed latency cost, the only way to make things feel fast everywhere is to shorten the physical distance — put copies of data near the user.

Without CDN:   user (Mumbai) ───── 250 ms ─────► origin server (US)   slow

With CDN:      user (Mumbai) ── 5 ms ──► edge node (Mumbai)          fast
                                              |  (cached copy)
                              origin (US) fills the cache occasionally
  • CDN edge nodes cache static content (images, video, JS) physically near users — the request never crosses an ocean.
  • Regional data centers / multi-region deployments place compute and data near major user populations.
  • This is why "design X for global scale" answers always include edge caching and regional replication — they're fighting the speed of light.
Full circle: encapsulation turns your message into bits → modulation turns bits into light → TIR carries that light through fiber across oceans → and because that journey has a physical time cost, we build CDNs to keep the journey short.
